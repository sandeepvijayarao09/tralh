Heritage

The Mughal Court

The Mughal Empire was founded by the Timurid prince Babur in 1526 after the First Battle of Panipat, reached its height under Akbar, Shah Jahan and Aurangzeb, and ended in 1857 when the British exiled the last emperor, Bahadur Shah Zafar. The house takes its name from Fatehpur Sikri, Akbar's red-sandstone capital beside Agra, a UNESCO World Heritage Site of 1986. A closed chapter; the craft endures.

Savoir-Faire

The craft beneath the house.

Pietra dura (parchin kari) marble inlay, seen at the UNESCO-listed Taj Mahal; Mughal miniature painting in gold and mineral pigment; kani pashmina and jamawar weaving, holding Indian Geographical Indication status (2008); pierced jali stone lattice within the Mughal monuments; and kundan gold-foil gem setting with meenakari enamel, refined in the imperial karkhanas.

The House

What Fatehpur makes.

A house of white-marble inlay, pierced stone lattice, woven pashmina and kundan-set gold — drawing on red sandstone, marble white and emerald green for a restrained, garden-cool imperial palette honouring a closed Mughal chapter.
