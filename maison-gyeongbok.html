Heritage

The Joseon Court

The House of Yi founded the Joseon dynasty in 1392 and built Gyeongbokgung as its main palace in Seoul. Its cultural peak fell in the fifteenth century under King Sejong; in 1897 the realm became the Korean Empire. The dynasty ended with the Japanese annexation of Korea in 1910, Sunjong its last reigning monarch — a closed chapter whose crafts endure.

Savoir-Faire

The craft beneath the house.

Court craft spanned najeon chilgi mother-of-pearl lacquerware and white porcelain (baekja), adopted as imperial ware under Sejong with royal kilns at Gwangju. Women of rank wore the norigae pendant, knotted in maedeup silk-cord work; palaces and temples carried dancheong polychrome painting. Najeon chilgi and maedeup are designated Korean intangible cultural properties.

The House

What Gyeongbok makes.

Lacquer, white porcelain and silk ornament in the Joseon register — mother-of-pearl inlay, baekja ceramics and knotted norigae. The palette pairs porcelain white with dancheong blue-green and gold: Confucian restraint lifted by court colour.
