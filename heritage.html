Heritage as Structure

Nine royal traditions,
held as one.

The cultural axes are not a theme; they are the permanent pillars of the House. Each is a verifiable craft lineage, gathered, codified, and renewed — never pastiched.

The Nine Cultural Axes

A grammar for every court.

The House bears no founding year of its own — it inherits the dates of the courts it unites, each a living lineage centuries old.

House of Windsor · since 1917

British Royal

The reigning House of Windsor — Highland and Tudor archive, tartan codes, Savile Row construction.

Imperial House · since 660 BC

Japanese Imperial

The world's oldest reigning dynasty — chrysanthemum codes, yūzen, maki-e, shibori.

House of Bourbon · since 1700

Spanish Royal

The reigning Bourbon-Spain and the Moorish palace tradition — brocade, Toledo metalwork, heraldry.

House of Grimaldi · since 1297

Monégasque Royal

The reigning Grimaldi court of Monaco — Riviera elegance, Belle Époque gilding, ceremonial orders.

Alaouite dynasty · since 1631

Moroccan Royal

The reigning Alaouite court — zellij geometry, gold thread, the caftan and ceremonial bisht.

House of Saud · since 1744

Gulf Royal

The reigning Saudi and Gulf courts — falconry heritage, oud and amber, gold-thread bisht.

Chakri dynasty · since 1782

Thai Royal

The reigning Chakri court — gold thread, royal Thai silk, temple geometry.

House of Mewar · since 734

Indian Royal

Udaipur's House of Mewar — among the world's longest-living dynasties; jadau, zardozi, katan silk.

Asante kingdom · since 1701

Asante Royal

The reigning Asante court of Ghana — kente weaving, gold regalia, adinkra symbols.

The Archive

A cultural institution, held in trust.

Every object, every material lot, every artisan credential is recorded permanently. The Archive is held by a charitable trust — it cannot be liquidated in any corporate event. The heritage outlives the House's every season.

Royal Patronage

Royal houses are stakeholders,
not sponsors.

Each allied royal house holds an annual right to nominate creators from its own tradition. Their year of work is attributed under that house's name and archived permanently — a living instrument of heritage, not a borrowed past.
