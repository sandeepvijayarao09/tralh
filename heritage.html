Heritage as Structure

The world's royal courts,
held as one.

Each royal house is a permanent pillar of the House — a court, living or historical, whose craft lineage we gather, codify, and renew, never pastiche. Not under one roof, but each in its own name.

The Royal Houses

A grammar for every court.

The House bears no founding year of its own — it inherits the dates of the courts it unites, each a lineage centuries old.

The living courts — fifteen

Abu Dhabi · since 1761

House of Al Nahyan

Al Sadu Bedouin weaving and falconry — both UNESCO-listed — and the gold-thread bisht. The court behind Qasr Al Watan and the Louvre Abu Dhabi; the falcon is the nation's emblem.

Dubai · since 1833

House of Al Maktoum

A pearling and dhow-trade lineage born on Dubai Creek; falconry and the saluki hound. The Al Fahidi quarter still keeps its wind-tower (barjeel) heritage.

Saudi Arabia · since 1727

House of Saud

The gold-embroidered bisht of Al-Ahsa, Al Sadu weaving, and the Ardah. Seat of At-Turaif in Diriyah — a UNESCO World Heritage mudbrick capital; the Kingdom unified in 1932.

Qatar · since 1878

House of Al Thani

A pearling and falconry heritage from Al Zubarah (UNESCO). Today a court of patronage — I. M. Pei's Museum of Islamic Art and Mathaf in Doha.

Oman · since 1744

House of Al Said

The silver khanjar and Omani silverwork; Dhofar frankincense (the UNESCO Land of Frankincense). Patron of the Royal Opera House Muscat.

Kuwait · since 1752

House of Al Sabah

Al Sadu weaving (UNESCO 2020), safeguarded by Kuwait's Sadu House; the dhow and pearling heritage; the diwaniya.

Bahrain · since 1783

House of Al Khalifa

The natural pearls of the Gulf and the Pearling Path of Muharraq (UNESCO); the Dilmun harbour of Qal'at al-Bahrain.

Morocco · since 1631

Alaouite Dynasty

Zellij mosaic, the Moroccan caftan (UNESCO 2025), babouche, and leather from the Chouara tannery of Fez. A sharifian house — the king is "Commander of the Faithful."

United Kingdom · since 1917

House of Windsor

The Crown Jewels and Imperial State Crown at the Tower; coronation robes by Ede & Ravenscroft (1689); royal warrants to Garrard, Asprey and Launer; Savile Row.

Spain · since 1700

House of Bourbon

The Royal Tapestry Factory (founded 1720); Loewe, royal warrant since 1905; the mantilla and peineta. Seat: the Royal Palace of Madrid.

Monaco · since 1297

House of Grimaldi

Europe's oldest reigning house; the Order of Saint-Charles (1858); Charles Garnier's Belle Époque Monte-Carlo. The Prince's Palace — wed to Grace Kelly, 1956.

Japan · since 660 BC

The Imperial House

The world's oldest monarchy and its venerated Three Sacred Treasures. Court crafts kept by Living National Treasures — yūzen dyeing, maki-e lacquer, nishijin-ori, the kimono.

Thailand · since 1782

The Chakri Dynasty

Royal Thai silk, revived by Queen Sirikit's SUPPORT Foundation; benjarong porcelain and nielloware. The Grand Palace and the Emerald Buddha of Wat Phra Kaew.

Udaipur, India · since 734

House of Mewar

The Sisodia line of Maharana Pratap — among the world's longest-serving dynasties; the Mewar school of miniature painting; meenakari and kundan. Seat: the City Palace, Udaipur.

Ghana · since 1701

The Asante Court

Kente woven at Bonwire, adinkra symbols, and Asante goldsmithing; the sacred Golden Stool (Sika Dwa Kofi). Seat: the Manhyia Palace, Kumasi.

Historical houses — six

Courts whose craft outlived the crown.

Six dynasties no longer reign. Their courts are closed, but their craft endures — gathered and renewed under the House's Archive, in tribute, and without claim on any living descendant.

Agra, India · 1526–1857

The Mughal Court

The Timurid emperors from Babur to Bahadur Shah Zafar. Pietra dura marble inlay of the Taj Mahal (UNESCO 1983), Mughal miniature, kani pashmina (GI 2008), jali lattice and kundan-set gold.

Hyderabad, India · 1724–1948

The Asaf Jahi Nizams

The Nizams of the Deccan. Golconda diamonds — the Koh-i-Noor and Hope among them — Bidriware silver inlay (GI 2006), Himroo and mashru brocade, and pearled zardozi.

Seoul, Korea · 1392–1910

The Joseon Court · House of Yi

Five centuries from King Sejong's golden age. Najeon chilgi mother-of-pearl lacquer, baekja white porcelain, dancheong polychrome, and the knotted norigae — designated Korean intangible cultural properties.

St Petersburg, Russia · 1613–1917

The House of Romanov

Three imperial centuries. Fabergé guilloché enamel and the Imperial Easter Eggs (1885–1916), Ural malachite, the Imperial Porcelain Factory (1744), and gold-thread court dress.

Vienna, Austria · 1273–1918

The House of Habsburg

From Rudolf I to 1918. Vienna porcelain (1718, now Augarten), J. & L. Lobmeyr crystal (1823), and the or nué gold-thread embroidery of the Order of the Golden Fleece, held in the Imperial Treasury.

Gondar, Ethiopia · 1270–1974

The Solomonic Dynasty

Seven centuries claiming descent from Solomon and Sheba. Processional gold and silver crosses, tilet hand-woven cotton, Harari silver, and Ge'ez manuscripts on vellum. Seat: Fasil Ghebbi, Gondar (UNESCO).

The Archive

A cultural institution, held in trust.

Every object, every material lot, every artisan credential is recorded permanently. The Archive is held by a charitable trust — it cannot be liquidated in any corporate event. The heritage outlives the House's every season.

Royal Patronage

Royal houses are stakeholders,
not sponsors.

A living court is a stakeholder, not a sponsor: each holds an annual right to nominate creators from its own tradition, whose year of work is attributed under that house's name and archived permanently — a living instrument of heritage, not a borrowed past.

A historical house keeps no such seat. Its craft is revived and stewarded directly by the House's Archive, in tribute — never claiming the endorsement of a living descendant.
